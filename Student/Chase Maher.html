SuperFund- In Your Backyard

        In 1993 Erin Brockovich took on PG&E, a multi million if not billion dollar corporation who polluted water causing death for their own personal gain in Hinckley. Others have done this too, one example is Southern California Edison, a corporation that provides electricity to roughly fourteen million in California, nearly half the population of the state, contaminated groundwater and sludge in Visalia, California and likely other locations. The location in Visalia was The Utility Pole Yard, where they treated electric poles with wood preservatives. These preservatives were strong fungicides and pesticides. They seeped into groundwater and sludges on site. They are likely not fully gone. People should know when there is heavy pollution and the corporations who did it should take responsibility.

        Superfund sites are locations with a high amount of a hazardous waste that are on the National Priorities List. CERCLA, also known as Superfund, was made in response to the poor hazardous waste management in the 1970’s. The site was cleaned by a steam injection system, an on site water treatment facility, and other methods. When SCE forfeited the land they took 2,400 cubic yards of contaminated soil. The cleanup slowed down significantly dropped in 2000, going from around 160 pounds of soil recovered to 4 pounds per day. A slurry wall was also built to help prevent the spread of unwanted wastes in soil into more soil and groundwater. The Pole Yard location was labeled by the EPA a Superfund site in 1976. The cleanup was deemed finished in 2004. On site there were 3.4 million gallons of chemical wastes- Pentachlorophenol and Creosote.

        Pentachlorophenol is a fungicide and herbicide. Pentachlorophenol has effects on organ development, blood formation, the endocrine system, and reproduction (birth defects) according to the ATSDR and CDC. Some exposure can lead to high body heat due to extra heat production on a cellular level. After long amounts of exposure to low amounts, generally a workplace, it can harm the liver, kidneys, and nervous system. Fortunately it can be measured by levels in urine, blood, and tissues. On the site, there were 900,000 gallons of Pentachlorophenol. Workers and the cleanup crew were no doubt exposed for enough time for those effects to form, unless they were protected well.

        Creosote is a pesticide. A quote from the ATSDR (Agency for Toxic Substance and Disease Registry) Public Health Statement best explains the effects

These reports indicate that brief exposure to large amounts of coal tar creosote may result in a rash or severe irritation of the skin, chemical burns of the surfaces of the eye, convulsions and mental confusion, kidney or liver problems, unconsciousness, or even death (ATSDR).

         In an even longer term scenario:

Longer exposure to lower levels of coal tar creosote, coal tar, coal tar pitch or coal tar pitch volatiles by direct contact with the skin or by exposure to the vapors from these mixtures can also result in increased sensitivity to sunlight, damage to the cornea, and skin damage such as reddening, blistering, or peeling. Longer exposures to the vapors of the creosotes, coal tar, coal tar pitch, or coal tar pitch volatiles can also cause irritation of the respiratory tract (ATSDR).

        These imply that

 There was a massive 2,500,000 gallons of creosote at the Visalia Pole Yard Site. There is a moderate chance creosote related injuries could have occurred on site after operating for roughly four decades.

A large similarity between the two is the possible carcinogenic nature of these chemicals. As acknowledged by the ATSDR, Creosote can lead to testicular and skin cancer, and Pentachlorophenol is labeled a probable carcinogen by EPA and the American Cancer Society, based on research by the IARC, International Agency for Research on Cancer and the NTP, National Toxicology Program. In the case of Creosote, ATSDR acknowledges that it can spread through contact with recently treated surfaces as well as wood treatment plants. Some forms of creosote are carcinogenic. Pentachlorophenol isn’t guaranteed to be carcinogenic, but it is suspected to be tied to liver cancer as well as other locations (increased cancer risk and tumors in animals).

The substance may have effects on the central nervous system, kidneys, liver, lungs, immune system, thyroid. This substance is possibly carcinogenic to humans. Animal tests show that this substance possibly causes toxicity to human reproduction or development (CDC).

This is another source that states Pentachlorophenol and Creosote are probable carcinogens. “This substance is probably carcinogenic to humans (CDC).” This quote is on the toxicological profiles of both substances as well as “It is strongly advised that this substance does not enter the environment (CDC).“ on the Creosote page. Both are very dangerous to aquatic life as well as people. Also from the CDC, Pentachlorophenol contains dioxins, a known carcinogen and a component of Agent Orange according to the American Cancer Society, a powerful defoliant used in the Vietnam war.

        Interestingly, the liver cancer rates in Tulare County (the county Visalia is part of) had a oddly high liver cancer rate from about 1988 to 2000. On a map of liver cancer rates in the state, Tulare had a higher rate and more mortalities. Not all of these are inherently tied to the pollution from the Visalia Utility Pole Yard, but due to groundwater and sludge pollution some cases could be tied to contamination from Pentachlorophenol and Creosote. Due to the lack of documentation in the 1930’s to the 1970’s, determining this is difficult. A test on site (despite there already being a Geothermal survey that doesn’t address contaminant levels) could determine if there is still soil contamination there.

        The location of the Superfund site is not unused, it is the Visalia Transit Maintenance and Operations Facility. A waste disposal is also on site. Health and Safety Code 25233 implies that there shouldn’t be an increase over time of humans and/or animals exposed to significant contaminants. A soil test could show a violation of this or compliance, as there are more people on site now.

        SCE has been minimally transparent regarding their former site. The only information on the site is from the EPA. SCE wouldn’t hand out information that is inherently self-harmful for transparency’s sake. There was a public hearing about the Superfund cleanup and the old SCE

location, but it was not attended publically at all and no word got out to avoid awareness of the site and what was on site.

        If people know what is in their groundwater when near Superfund Sites and places with hazardous wastes, people could have a choice as well as knowledge of the issue. The responsible party needs to be held accountable for any issues related to the contaminants during or before the cleanup, as they introduced these things into the scenario.

        People should know what is in their soil and groundwater and responsible parties should be held accountable. Public knowledge of this gives people the choice to stay where they are knowing of the risks or to move out of concern. According to the ATSDR, a part of the Center for Disease Control, low levels of Pentachlorophenol and Creosote can cause harm after prolonged exposure, it would be sensible to move. If groundwater is polluted cleaning it up even as people drink it doesn’t reverse the side effects. Alternative wells could be used for public safety, even though at some points in time the Utility Pole Yard was not in the city of Visalia, but near still. Later it was in the city as Visalia grew, posing a potential hazard. Being poisoned from the groundwater unknowingly can lead to more situations like Erin Brockovich's where people died and one person had to stand up for everyone.

        Companies should take responsibility for their actions. If a company compensates for pollution, hazard-related illness/cancer, and hazard related mortality as well as forfeiting the land they used after cleanup is complete, transparency can increase even more as the public would know the company is responsible, and all damages can be repaid. If a person is handicapped or killed as a side effect of a company earning, the company should not just make money harming people. That was the case with Hinckley for the longest time, PG&E polluting groundwater until Erin Brockovich. It would be best if companies took care of issues like this

themselves, instead of someone having to devote time into researches and making a case, so         people could have compensation for damages that already occurred and knowledge of that damage.

        The transparency of the responsible party matters heavily as if they aren’t transparent about these issues they don’t go public. Even though it’s because of the self harm it entail, injury to people is not something a company should get away with just because they risk self-loss.

        A probable counter argument is that knowledge of these hazards will cause public paranoia. Some people may act irrationally, but laws are still in place and society is not collapsing, chemical hazards are in the soil and are being cleaned up. If people do not want to deal with the risk of contamination, they could relocate. An issue with that is cost, but the responsible party should cover relocation. Avoiding damage costs is in their best of interest if public knowledge exists, and relocation could be more sparing than compensation for death.

        Another likely counterargument is that they didn’t know the effects of the chemicals, to claim  non-negligence, as things of harm are constantly at the construction workplace, and waivers may prevent employees from taking action for injury compensation, but that doesn’t by any means apply to the public. At the same time to claim non-negligence isn’t valid, as the effects of both have been . So even if employees cannot receive compensation for injury/death, the public can receive compensation for damages.

        In conclusion, it would be best to run a geotechnical survey to find out the amounts of Creosote and Pentachlorophenol in the soil and groundwater and the amount in people to see if SCE needs to take responsibility for anything after the Superfund Site was finished, cleaned up, and beforehand.
